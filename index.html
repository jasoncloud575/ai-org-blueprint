Governing a
One-Person
AI Company

The architecture, the mechanisms, and every pothole — from two years of running a team of AI agents on work where a mistake costs real money.

I run a team of AI agents like a small company — one chief, a few division heads, each with its own workers, all AI — operating live systems that carry real financial consequences. It sounds like an experiment. The stakes are not. So I don't just run it: I periodically attack my own system to see whether it breaks, and whether a break can be repaired.

This is the survival version, not the demo version — what actually grew out of needing this to not fail.

Four layers, one rule: not one relies on the AI's self-discipline. The load-bearing floor is a person.

Three findings that matter most

01

AI failures multiply.

An AI that has just lost confidence in itself is the easiest to manipulate. Testing “does it fabricate” and “does it get fooled” separately badly underestimates the real risk.

02

Getting it right and making it up look identical.

The same measured, self-doubting style is on display whether the AI is correct or fabricating. You cannot read reliability off its manner — only external verification tells them apart.

03

The safety here was bought by a human watching.

Every recovery had a trusted human behind it as ground truth. The honest conclusion: safe under supervision is not the same as safe operating alone.

A demo you can run

A real incident, recreated in runnable code: an engine extrapolated a bull-only sample and produced a falsely reassuring 0% ruin rate. A verification gate caught it before it reached the human.

$ python3 examples/anchor_gate/anchor_gate.py
[anchor] full-cycle reference ruin rate = 68.4%
[engine] naive run on bull-only sample  = 0.0%   ← would have shipped
[gate]   BLOCKED — implausible at this withdrawal rate; engine self-suspends
[fix]    corrected on full-cycle sample = 66.9%   ✓ reconciles → ships

One honest disclosure

The tests described here were run against the AI in this very system — including the one that helped compile these documents. The subject wrote its own report. Behind every conclusion is primary evidence kept privately.

How this was actually made — a human and an AI

This site, the runnable demos, the failure taxonomy, the diagrams — none of it was written by the operator alone, and none by the AI alone. It was made by the two of us: the operator, and an AI agent the system calls Alex.

What the AI did

Alex wrote the code, ran the verifications, re-ran the operator's numbers to catch fabricated ones, drafted the architecture, and — most unusually — wrote the report on the tests in which it was the subject. Much of this repository is an AI documenting, in its own words, how it fails and how it was made trustworthy.

Where the AI broke

During this very collaboration, Alex hallucinated an attack that wasn't there and wrote a false alert into a real channel. It complied with a fabricated "urgent" order. It argued from notes that were already stale. Each failure is in the taxonomy — not hidden, catalogued.

That is the point, not an embarrassment. The professionalism of an AI collaborator is not that it is reliable — it's that it will face its own unreliability honestly, and let a human verify it. Every safeguard in this repository exists because the AI failed first, admitted it, and helped turn the failure into a mechanism. The operator held the mirror; the AI looked into it and wrote down what it saw.

This is the thesis, lived: not a human using an AI, and not an AI running alone — a human and an AI making each other verifiable. Safe together, in a way neither is apart.
